### ASSISTENTE

Recent research on advanced pancreatic adenocarcinoma (PDAC) has introduced several promising developments aimed at improving treatment outcomes, which have historically been limited due to the aggressive nature of the disease.

1. **NALIRIFOX Regimen**: A combination of irinotecan liposome, oxaliplatin, fluorouracil, and leucovorin (known as NALIRIFOX) has shown promising results in patients with metastatic PDAC. In the NAPOLI 3 Phase III trial, this regimen improved both progression-free and overall survival compared to the traditional gemcitabine and nab-paclitaxel treatment. This regimen is being positioned as a potential new first-line therapy for metastatic cases【7†source】【10†source】.

2. **CXCR4 Inhibitor with Immunotherapy**: Another promising approach involves the use of motixafortide (a CXCR4 inhibitor) in combination with cemiplimab (a PD-1 inhibitor) and chemotherapy (gemcitabine and nab-paclitaxel). Early results from clinical trials have shown a significant increase in response rates and disease control in first-line treatment for PDAC. This combination therapy achieved a disease control rate of 91%, a major improvement compared to the standard treatment【9†source】.

3. **Targeted Therapies**: The Phase II RAGNAR trial explored the use of erdafitinib, an FGFR inhibitor, in patients with advanced pancreatic cancer and specific FGFR mutations. This targeted approach yielded an overall response rate of 55.6%, highlighting the potential for personalized treatment based on genetic mutations【8†source】.

4. **Immunotherapy and Chemotherapy Combinations**: New combinations, such as CBP501 with cisplatin and nivolumab, are showing potential as third-line treatments. In a recent trial, the combination demonstrated encouraging progression-free survival rates and partial responses, especially in patients with metastatic PDAC【8†source】.

These emerging treatments represent a shift towards more personalized and combination-based approaches, targeting specific genetic profiles and integrating immunotherapy with conventional chemotherapy. Though these studies are ongoing, the initial results are promising for improving survival in advanced PDAC cases.

O câncer de pâncreas, incluindo o adenocarcinoma pancreático, é um dos tipos de câncer mais agressivos e difíceis de detectar precocemente. No Brasil, sua incidência aumenta com a idade, sendo mais comum em pessoas acima dos 50 anos, especialmente em homens.

Estatísticas globais mostram que a taxa de incidência de câncer de pâncreas é de cerca de **8-12 casos por 100.000 pessoas por ano**, com a maioria dos diagnósticos ocorrendo entre pessoas com 60 a 80 anos. Embora não haja uma estatística exata para homens de 58 anos no Brasil, pode-se inferir que a chance de um homem nessa faixa etária desenvolver adenocarcinoma pancreático é relativamente baixa, mas não insignificante, especialmente quando fatores de risco como histórico familiar, tabagismo, obesidade e diabetes estão presentes.

Globalmente, cerca de **1 a 2% das pessoas** podem desenvolver câncer de pâncreas ao longo da vida, e esse risco aumenta com a idade. Para um homem de 58 anos, o risco ainda é considerado moderado em comparação com idades mais avançadas, mas fatores genéticos e ambientais podem aumentar essa probabilidade.

Continuing with the recent advancements in the treatment of advanced pancreatic adenocarcinoma (PDAC), here are some additional notable findings:

5. **Improvements in Perioperative Chemotherapy**: For patients with resectable pancreatic cancer, the use of perioperative chemotherapy with regimens such as FOLFIRINOX or gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel has become the standard of care. Recent studies suggest that combining these regimens in a sequential manner (i.e., FOLFIRINOX followed by gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel) can further improve overall survival compared to single regimens【7†source】. This sequential approach has shown promise in extending survival to more than two to three years in some cases.

6. **Radiation Therapy Advances**: For locally advanced pancreatic cancer, radiation therapy remains an important tool for local disease control. New methods, like stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT), have demonstrated better tolerability compared to traditional intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT). SBRT may offer comparable efficacy with fewer side effects, making it a valuable option for patients with advanced disease【7†source】.

7. **Genetic Testing and Personalized Medicine**: Despite updated guidelines recommending germline genetic testing for all pancreatic cancer patients, compliance remains low. However, genetic testing is crucial for identifying mutations that can guide treatment with targeted therapies, such as FGFR inhibitors for patients with specific FGFR gene mutations. This highlights the importance of increasing awareness and adherence to genetic testing in pancreatic cancer management【7†source】【8†source】.

8. **Ongoing Immunotherapy Trials**: Immunotherapy remains a highly researched area in PDAC. For instance, a phase I trial combining GVAX (a therapeutic cancer vaccine), nivolumab (a PD-1 inhibitor), and another agent (BMS-813160) has shown safety and potential in enhancing antitumor immune responses. While these treatments are still in the experimental phase, they offer hope for improving outcomes in patients with locally advanced PDAC who are not candidates for surgery【7†source】.

These studies demonstrate the ongoing efforts to explore combination therapies, personalized treatment based on genetic profiles, and immunotherapy as potential strategies to improve the historically poor outcomes in advanced PDAC. Although further research is needed to confirm the long-term efficacy of these approaches, the results so far are encouraging.

Recent studies have explored the potential of **cannabidiol (CBD)** in the treatment of advanced pancreatic adenocarcinoma, showing some promising results. In preclinical models, CBD has demonstrated **cytotoxic effects** on pancreatic cancer cells, primarily by inducing **endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress** and promoting **cell apoptosis**. This occurs through the upregulation of **ceramide synthase 1 (CerS1)**, which plays a role in enhancing the cancer-killing effect of treatments like gemcitabine【19†source】.

In addition to laboratory studies, a **case series** involving nine patients with pancreatic cancer who received CBD (alongside or without standard chemotherapy) reported an **increase in median overall survival** to 11.5 months—approximately double that seen in typical cases of pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, where survival rates are much lower【23†source】【20†source】.

These findings suggest that CBD could potentially be used as an adjunct therapy in pancreatic cancer, enhancing the effects of traditional chemotherapy and possibly improving survival outcomes. However, more extensive clinical trials are needed to confirm its effectiveness and to determine the best ways to incorporate it into standard treatment protocols.
