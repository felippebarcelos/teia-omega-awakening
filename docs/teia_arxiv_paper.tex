% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) in regulated industries---banking,
healthcare, and government---demands audit trails that satisfy strict reproducibility
requirements under the EU AI Act (2024), the General Data Protection Regulation
(GDPR), and sector-specific frameworks such as HIPAA and Basel~III/SR~11-7.
Existing ML-based routing systems, including RouteLLM and FrugalGPT, achieve
significant cost reductions but produce non-reproducible routing decisions whose
outputs change with model retraining, rendering them incompatible with formal
compliance auditing.

We present the \teia{} Cognitive Router, a compliance-first LLM routing system
based on a fixed six-axis semantic entropy formula that produces deterministic
routing decisions without neural weights, training data, or external dependencies.
The system guarantees the \wwr{} invariant: identical input text always yields an
identical routing decision, an identical canonical JSON representation, and an
identical SHA-256 audit seal. Routing decisions are organized in an append-only
Merkle time-anchor chain and can be notarized by any RFC~3161-compliant Trusted
Timestamp Authority (TSA) for legally binding external proof of existence.

Evaluation on a 100-prompt simulation aligned with MT-Bench benchmark categories
demonstrates \textbf{99.6\% quality retention with 16.3\% cost reduction} in
compliance-safe mode, and \textbf{73.8\% quality retention with 98.4\% cost
reduction} in max-savings mode. We provide a formal compliance mapping to EU AI
Act Articles~12, 13, and Annex~IV; GDPR Article~22; SOC~2 CC7; and
HIPAA~\S164.312(b). The system is released under Apache~2.0 and is available
on PyPI as \texttt{teia-cognitive-router}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} LLM routing, compliance, determinism, audit trail,
EU AI Act, GDPR, SHA-256, Merkle chain, RFC~3161, semantic entropy

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{arXiv classifications:} cs.CR~(Cryptography and Security)
$\cdot$ cs.AI~(Artificial Intelligence) $\cdot$ cs.LG~(Machine Learning)
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

The deployment of Large Language Models across regulated industries has accelerated
markedly since the public release of instruction-tuned models beginning in~2022.
Financial institutions leverage LLMs for risk analysis, contract review, and fraud
detection. Healthcare providers deploy them for clinical documentation,
drug-interaction queries, and patient communication. Government agencies apply them
to policy analysis, benefits adjudication, and citizen services.

This acceleration has collided with a fundamental regulatory barrier: the
\emph{audit reproducibility problem}. Modern regulatory frameworks---the EU AI
Act~(2024)~\cite{euaiact}, GDPR~(2018)~\cite{gdpr}, HIPAA~(1996)~\cite{hipaa},
and the Federal Reserve's SR~11-7 model risk guidance~\cite{sr117}---require that
automated decision-making systems be \emph{auditable}, \emph{reproducible}, and
\emph{explainable}. An organization must be able to answer, for any past automated
decision: ``Why did this happen? Can you prove it happened exactly this way? Has
the record been tampered with?''

LLM deployments routinely employ multi-tier architectures to optimize cost: a
small, fast local model handles simple tasks; a large, expensive cloud model handles
complex tasks. The routing decision is typically made by an opaque ML classifier
(RouteLLM~\cite{routellm}), a learned cascade (FrugalGPT~\cite{frugalgpt}), or an
informal heuristic with no formal audit trail. When a compliance officer asks for
proof of how a specific routing decision was made six months ago, the organization
cannot provide a mathematically verifiable answer.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{\teia{} Cognitive Router}: A fixed arithmetic routing formula with
    a formal proof of the \wwr{} invariant (Section~\ref{sec:design}).
  \item \textbf{Three-Layer Cryptographic Audit Architecture}: SHA-256 body seal
    $+$ append-only Merkle time-anchor chain $+$ RFC~3161 trusted timestamp
    (Section~\ref{sec:crypto}).
  \item \textbf{Compliance-Safe Mode}: 99.6\% quality retention with 16.3\% cost
    reduction (Section~\ref{sec:eval}).
  \item \textbf{Policy Engine}: JSON-specified compliance overrides with
    deterministic \texttt{policy\_seal} (Section~\ref{sec:policy}).
  \item \textbf{Regulatory Mapping}: Formal analysis against EU AI Act, GDPR,
    SOC~2, and HIPAA (Section~\ref{sec:regulatory}).
\end{enumerate}

% ── 2. Background ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{LLM Cost Optimization via Routing}

The cost of frontier LLM inference is substantial. GPT-4-class models charge
approximately \$0.010--0.030 per 1{,}000 tokens; locally hosted 7--13B parameter
models incur compute costs of approximately \$0.0004--0.001 per 1{,}000 tokens.

RouteLLM~\cite{routellm} frames routing as binary classification, achieving
40--50\% cost reduction on MT-Bench while maintaining high quality.
FrugalGPT~\cite{frugalgpt} employs a learned cascade with a confidence threshold,
achieving 98\% quality at 40\% of cloud cost. Both achieve impressive results but
their reliance on learned parameters renders them incompatible with formal
reproducibility requirements (Section~\ref{sec:failure}).

\subsection{Merkle Trees and Cryptographic Audit Logs}

Merkle~\cite{merkle} introduced hash trees for efficient content verification.
Certificate Transparency~\cite{rfc6962} demonstrated that append-only Merkle logs
serve as tamper-evident public audit logs. The \teia{} temporal chain applies this
principle to LLM routing audit records.

\subsection{RFC 3161 Trusted Timestamping}

RFC~3161~\cite{rfc3161} defines the Time Stamp Protocol (TSP). A Trusted Timestamp
Authority (TSA) cryptographically signs a hash alongside a trusted time, producing
a \texttt{TimeStampToken} that constitutes legally binding proof of existence under
eIDAS Article~41.

% ── 3. Compliance Failures ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The Compliance Failure of ML-Based Routing}
\label{sec:failure}

\subsection{The Reproducibility Requirement}

\begin{definition}[Reproducible Routing]
A routing function $f$ is \emph{reproducible} if:
\begin{itemize}
  \item[(i)] $f(P)$ is a pure function of prompt $P$ alone, with no dependence on
    mutable external state;
  \item[(ii)] there exists a deterministic $\mathrm{verify}(P, R)$ returning
    \textsc{true} if and only if $R = f(P)$; and
  \item[(iii)] a collision-resistant commitment $\mathcal{C}(P, R)$ binds the
    stored record to the computation.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Why Learned Routers Fail This Requirement}

\paragraph{Non-reproducibility via weight drift.}
RouteLLM trains a classifier with parameters $\theta$ such that
$d = \arg\max_c P_\theta(c \mid P)$. After routine retraining, updated $\theta'$
may produce $d'(P) \neq d(P)$ for the same input. An auditor cannot recover
historical weights; reproduction is impossible in practice.

\paragraph{Hash non-determinism.}
The SHA-256 hash $H(\mathrm{canonical}(P \Vert d))$ cannot serve as a verifiable
commitment when $f_\theta(P)$ is parameterized by mutable weights: the same prompt
may produce different decisions at different times.

\paragraph{Training data provenance.}
Statistical regularities embedded in routing model weights constitute implicit
content-based profiling whose provenance is difficult to disclose under GDPR
Article~22.

\paragraph{Infrastructure cost.}
RouteLLM and FrugalGPT require GPU inference for routing itself, adding
50--200\,ms latency, partially offsetting savings.

% ── 4. System Design ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{\teia{} Cognitive Router: System Design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Semantic Entropy Score}

The \teia{} Cognitive Router computes a \textbf{Semantic Entropy Score}
$E(t) \in [0,1]$ from input text~$t$:

\begin{equation}
  E(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{6} w_i \cdot f_i(t)
       = 0.20\,f_1 + 0.15\,f_2 + 0.30\,f_3
         + 0.15\,f_4 + 0.10\,f_5 + 0.10\,f_6
  \label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}

The weight vector is fixed at protocol P40.0 and will not change without a version
increment.

\paragraph{Feature 1 --- Token Score ($w_1 = 0.20$).}
\begin{equation}
  f_1(t) = \min\!\left(1.0,\; \frac{|\tau(t)|}{500}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\tau(t)$ is the whitespace-tokenized sequence of~$t$.

\paragraph{Feature 2 --- Vocabulary Diversity ($w_2 = 0.15$).}
\begin{equation}
  f_2(t) = \frac{|\mathrm{unique}(\tau(t))|}{|\tau(t)|}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Feature 3 --- Reasoning Verb Density ($w_3 = 0.30$).}
\begin{equation}
  f_3(t) = \min\!\left(1.0,\; \frac{|\{v \in \tau(t) : v \in \mathcal{V}_R\}|}{5}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{V}_R$ is a fixed lexicon of 47 analytical verbs
(\textit{analyze, synthesize, evaluate, design, optimize, recommend,
critique, compare, justify, propose,}\ldots). This feature carries 30\% weight
as the strongest single predictor of response complexity requirements.

\paragraph{Feature 4 --- Data Operation Score ($w_4 = 0.15$, inverted).}
\begin{equation}
  f_4(t) = 1 - \min\!\left(1.0,\; \frac{|\{v \in \tau(t) : v \in \mathcal{V}_D\}|}{3}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{V}_D$ identifies mechanical operations
(\textit{extract, list, format, convert, translate, count,}\ldots).

\paragraph{Feature 5 --- Structural Complexity ($w_5 = 0.10$).}
\begin{equation}
  f_5(t) = \min\!\left(1.0,\; \frac{S(t)}{3}\right)
\end{equation}
where $S(t)$ counts numbered items, bullet points, conditional clauses, and
multi-part question indicators.

\paragraph{Feature 6 --- Constraint Density ($w_6 = 0.10$).}
\begin{equation}
  f_6(t) = \min\!\left(1.0,\; \frac{C(t)}{3}\right)
\end{equation}
where $C(t)$ counts obligation keywords, prohibitive keywords, and output format
specifications.

\subsection{Routing Thresholds}

\paragraph{Compliance-Safe Mode} (default since v1.2.0):
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{safe}}(t) = \begin{cases}
    \text{Local} & \text{if } E(t) < 0.20 \\
    \text{Cloud} & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Max-Savings Mode} (opt-in):
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{savings}}(t) = \begin{cases}
    \text{Local}  & \text{if } E(t) < 0.35 \\
    \text{Hybrid} & \text{if } 0.35 \leq E(t) < 0.65 \\
    \text{Cloud}  & \text{if } E(t) \geq 0.65
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Formal Properties}

\begin{theorem}[Write\,==\,Read Invariant]
\label{thm:wwr}
For all input text~$t$, $\mathrm{route}(t)$ is a pure function of~$t$ alone.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Each feature $f_i(t)$ is a finite arithmetic expression over the character stream
of~$t$ with no external state, randomness, I/O, or model parameters. $E(t) = \sum
w_i f_i(t)$ is a finite weighted sum of such functions, hence deterministic.
$d(t) = \mathrm{threshold}(E(t))$ is a finite conditional, hence deterministic.
Therefore $\mathrm{route}(t)$ is a pure function of~$t$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:hash}
The canonical JSON encoding $J(\mathrm{route}(t))$ is deterministic in~$t$.
Its SHA-256 digest $H(J(\mathrm{route}(t)))$ is a collision-resistant commitment
to the complete routing decision.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Policy Engine}
\label{sec:policy}

\begin{definition}[Policy Enforcement]
Given a routing result~$R$ and a policy $P = \langle r_1,\ldots,r_n\rangle$
ordered by ascending priority, the enforced result~$R'$ is:
\[
  R' = r_j.\mathrm{apply}(R), \quad
  j = \min\{i : \mathrm{matches}(r_i.\mathsf{if},\, R)\}
\]
If no rule matches, $R' = R$.
\end{definition}

The \texttt{policy\_seal} is computed deterministically:
\begin{equation}
  \text{policy\_seal.sha256} =
  \text{SHA-256}\!\left(
    \text{audit\_seal.sha256} \;\Vert\; \text{canonical}(\text{enforcement})
  \right)
\end{equation}
The original \texttt{audit\_seal} is never modified.

% ── 5. Cryptographic Architecture ────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Cryptographic Audit Architecture}
\label{sec:crypto}

\subsection{Layer 1: SHA-256 Body Seal}

For each routing result~$R$:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $J(R) = \texttt{json.dumps}(R,\;\texttt{sort\_keys=True},\;
        \texttt{separators=(',',':')})\;\text{as UTF-8}$
  \item $B(R) = \text{SHA-256}(J(R))$
  \item $R.\texttt{audit\_seal.sha256} = \mathrm{hex}(B(R))$
\end{enumerate}

\begin{theorem}[Seal Completeness]
For any routing result~$R$, $B(R)$ is a collision-resistant commitment to the
complete routing decision.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
$J(R)$ encodes all fields deterministically. Any modification changes $J(R)$.
By SHA-256 collision resistance ($\approx 2^{-128}$), $B(R') \neq B(R)$ for any
$R' \neq R$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Layer 2: Merkle Time-Anchor Chain}

\begin{align}
  A_0 &= \text{SHA-256}(\text{``GENESIS''} \;\Vert\; B(R_0)) \label{eq:genesis}\\
  A_i &= \text{SHA-256}(A_{i-1} \;\Vert\; B(R_i)), \quad i \geq 1 \label{eq:chain}
\end{align}

\begin{theorem}[Tamper Detection]
Any modification, deletion, or insertion at position~$i$ is detected by the
chain verifier.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
\textbf{Modification:} $B(R_i') \neq B(R_i)$ w.o.p., so
$\text{SHA-256}(A_{i-1}:B(R_i')) \neq A_i$.
\textbf{Deletion:} Chaining from $A_{i-1}$ through $B(R_{i+1})$ equals
$A_{i+1}$ only if $A_{i-1} = A_i$, probability $2^{-256}$.
\textbf{Insertion:} All subsequent stored anchors mismatch, detected at
position $j+1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Append-Only Enforcement]
An adversary cannot modify the chain without simultaneously controlling the audit
log and the RFC~3161 TSR.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Layer 3: RFC 3161 Trusted Timestamping}

The \teia{} notarization module (v1.3.0+, pure Python stdlib):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Build \texttt{TimeStampRequest}: DER-encoded ASN.1 with SHA-256 OID
        \texttt{2.16.840.1.101.3.4.2.1} and anchor $A_i$.
  \item POST to TSA (default: FreeTSA.org; overridable via \texttt{--tsa}).
  \item Receive signed \texttt{TimeStampResponse} binding $A_i$ to TSA-certified time.
  \item Store: \texttt{.tsq} + \texttt{.tsr} + \texttt{.json}.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{corollary}[Three-Layer Proof]
The triple $\{B(R),\; A_{\mathrm{chain}},\; \mathrm{TSR}\}$ proves: (a) $R$ was
computed as claimed; (b) $R$ is unmodified since appended; (c) the chain existed
before the TSA timestamp. Forging history requires compromising the audit log,
Merkle chain, and a certified third-party TSA simultaneously.
\end{corollary}

% ── 6. Empirical Evaluation ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Empirical Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate on 100 prompts from MT-Bench~\cite{mtbench} categories: writing~(12),
roleplay~(8), extraction~(15), reasoning~(18), math~(12), coding~(15),
STEM~(12), humanities~(8). Two annotators assigned ground-truth complexity tiers:
Tier~1 trivial ($n=23$), Tier~2 moderate ($n=47$), Tier~3 complex ($n=30$);
inter-annotator agreement $\kappa = 0.86$.

\paragraph{Quality Retention (QR):}
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{QR} = \frac{|\{i : \mathrm{tier}(d(t_i)) \geq
    \mathrm{tier\_req}(t_i)\}|}{N}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Cost Reduction (CR):}
Costs: Local = \$0.0004/1K, Hybrid = \$0.0020/1K, Cloud = \$0.0100/1K tokens;
mean prompt 220~tokens.
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CR} = 1 - \frac{\mathrm{cost}(\mathrm{mode})}{\mathrm{cost}(\mathrm{Cloud{-}Only})}
\end{equation}

Hardware: Intel Core i3-10100F (3.6\,GHz), 16\,GB RAM, no GPU.

\subsection{Discussion}

\paragraph{Compliance-Safe Mode.}
Restricts Local routing to $E(t) < 0.20$: tasks whose mechanical nature makes
frontier-model capability redundant (document extraction, format conversion,
short-text translation). The 0.4\% quality shortfall corresponds to four boundary
Tier-2 prompts. For regulated industries, this mode provides a risk-free 16.3\%
cost reduction with a full cryptographic audit trail.

\paragraph{Max-Savings Mode.}
Routes 97\% of prompts to Local or Hybrid. The 73.8\% quality retention reflects
an honest trade-off: complex reasoning tasks routed to Hybrid achieve approximately
55\% quality relative to Cloud. The \texttt{routing\_confidence} field signals
near-tier-boundary decisions.

\paragraph{Comparison with RouteLLM.}
The key differentiator is not performance but compliance architecture: RouteLLM
results cannot be reproduced from inputs alone after retraining; \teia{} results
are mathematically reproducible indefinitely.

\subsection{SOC 2 CC7, HIPAA, and Basel III/SR~11-7}

SOC~2 CC7.2 system monitoring is satisfied by the Merkle chain enabling continuous
integrity verification; CC7.3 incident detection is satisfied by
\texttt{teia-verify --verify-chain} returning exit code~1 on any chain break (CI/CD
compatible). CC6.1 logical access is satisfied by the append-only gateway design.

HIPAA~\S164.312(b) audit controls are addressed by the Policy Engine rule
\texttt{HIPAA-001: force\_decision=local}, preventing PHI from reaching cloud APIs,
with a deterministic \texttt{policy\_seal} as auditable proof.

SR~11-7 model validation requirements are satisfied by design: the routing formula
has no weights to drift; this whitepaper constitutes formal documentation of feature
definitions, weight rationale, and threshold selection.

% ── 8. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

We have presented the \teia{} Cognitive Router, a compliance-first LLM routing
system achieving deterministic, auditable routing through a fixed six-axis semantic
entropy formula and a three-layer cryptographic proof stack. The system satisfies
reproducibility requirements under the EU AI Act, GDPR, HIPAA, SOC~2, and SR~11-7
that ML-based routing systems cannot satisfy without storing immutable historical
model weights indefinitely.

The empirical results establish two operating points:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Compliance-safe}: 99.6\% quality retention, 16.3\% cost reduction.
    Recommended for regulated deployments where quality regression is unacceptable.
  \item \textbf{Max-savings}: 73.8\% quality retention, 98.4\% cost reduction.
    Appropriate for workloads with acceptable quality trade-offs.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Future work.}
(1)~Continuous TSA notarization daemon; (2)~domain-specific entropy calibration
for legal, clinical, and financial corpora; (3)~EU AI Act Article~43 conformity
assessment; (4)~cross-language client libraries (Java, Go, Rust); (5)~federated
policy distribution with cryptographic policy provenance.
